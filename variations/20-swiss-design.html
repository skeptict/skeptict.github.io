The Grid as Foundation

Swiss design, also known as the International Typographic Style, emerged in the 1950s as a response to the chaos of post-war visual culture. Its principles are simple yet profound: mathematical grids, sans-serif typography, asymmetric layouts, and objective photography. The grid isn't a constraint—it's a framework for clarity. Every element aligns to a rational structure, creating harmony through systematic organization. This approach prioritized readability and functionality above all else, believing that design should communicate, not decorate.

Helvetica and Hierarchy

The typography of Swiss design favors neutrality and legibility. Helvetica, developed in 1957, became the movement's iconic typeface—clean, objective, universal. Visual hierarchy is achieved through size, weight, and spacing rather than decorative flourishes. The result is timeless. What worked in print posters seventy years ago still works on digital screens today. This is design stripped to its essence: clear communication through rational structure. No trends, no gimmicks, just pure information architecture.
